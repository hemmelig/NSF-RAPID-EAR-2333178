# Project Outcomes Report for NSF Project 2333178

The movement of magma within volcanic systems can have an effect on the strength of the local magnetic field in a number of ways. If these magnetic changes can be measured reliably, they could offer an additional way to monitor volcanoes for changes in activity. This would complement existing techniques like GNSS, gravity surveys, and seismic monitoring. This project set out to test the viability of magnetic monitoring at Askja, a large caldera-forming volcano in the central highlands of Iceland, which began a new episode of inflation in 2021 after decades of gradual subsidence. This ongoing episode of inflation provides a rare opportunity to record magnetic field changes during a period of magmatic recharge.

In August 2023, we deployed a temporary network of magnetometers in and around the main caldera of Askja. This network included six high-resolution instruments provided by the EarthScope Primary Instrument Center, as well as eleven low-cost prototype magnetometers designed by project co-PI Dr. Joe Biasi from the University of Wyoming. These instruments recorded continuous, three-component magnetic field data for two years in a remote and challenging environment. During our field campaigns, we also conducted ground-based and aerial surveys with magnetometers that measured absolute field strengths in order to map the spatial variations in the magnetic field and provide additional context for the continuous measurements.

The deployment was highly successful, with more than 90% of the expected data were recovered over the two-year experiment. The project provided the first extended field deployment of the EPIC magnetometers, as well as testing the prototype coarse-resolution magnetometers. While these low-cost sensors were primarily experimental, the data they collected will guide improvements in their design and help determine whether inexpensive instruments could supplement or partially replace higher-cost magnetic sensors in future monitoring networks.

The primary scientific product of the project is the unique two-year dataset capturing magnetic field variations during an ongoing volcanic inflation episode. Analysis of these data is still underway. At present, no confirmed volcanic magnetic signal has been identified, but the dataset provides the foundation needed to test whether the models of the evolution of the magnetic field can be matched with the recorded data as well as other geophysical datasets recorded at Askja. The continuous measurements also reveal a strong preexisting magnetic anomaly within the caldera, which will help guide future investigations into the structure and evolution of the volcanic system.

The broader impacts of the project include extensive field training for early-career scientists, experience with drone-based aeromagnetic surveying, and strengthened collaborations between researchers in the United States and Iceland. The project has also generated openly available datasets and design insights that will be important for guiding future volcanic monitoring efforts in this field.
